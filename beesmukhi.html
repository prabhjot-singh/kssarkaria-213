§2 · Water pond

Beesmukhi

"Beesmukhi" (bees = twenty, mukhi = faced) is what its Bhojpuri-speaking sculptors called the concrete statue in this small water pond. It depicts — once we have sublimated those concrete rods into zero thickness edges in our boundless imagination — an icosahedron, that is, a regular solid having 20 equilateral triangular faces, with 5 faces at each of its (20 × 3) ÷ 5 = 12 vertices.

We also see, thanks to the omission of the faces, that its 12 = 3 × 4 vertices are the vertices of 3 identical rectangles, each inserted perpendicularly half-way through a parallel central slit of the next in cyclic order.

Three rectangles — or an icosahedron

Drag to rotate. The three rectangles are each a golden rectangle — length ÷ breadth = φ. Their twelve corners are exactly the twelve vertices of a regular icosahedron.

Why the ratio must be the golden one

To verify this construction gives an icosahedron, we note that the 12 vertices are equidistant from the common center of the 3 rectangles; and each is incident to 5 edges, while each edge has 2 vertices, so there are in all (12 × 5) ÷ 2 = 30 edges. Of these, the 24 which join a vertex to one of the two nearest vertices of the next rectangle have clearly, by symmetry, the same length a. Also, the remaining 6 edges, being the shorter sides of the identical rectangles, have the same length b.

In general, a is not equal to b. However, the above construction gives an icosahedron, provided the three identical rectangles have a special length-to-breadth ratio. For, if we keep b fixed, then a increases or decreases with l, the common length of the rectangles, being patently much bigger than b when the rectangles are very long, but is lesser than b when the rectangles approach 'squarehood' — so there is an optimal l at which a = b.

The above 'special' ratio turns out to be none other than the golden ratio!

In his own words: counting edges and faces

Here explicitly is the omitted general prescription for a regular solid {p, q}: its finitely many vertices should be on a sphere, its faces should be equal regular — i.e., with consecutive vertices equally spaced on a circle — polygons with p sides and vertices, and there should be q of these faces at each vertex. Clearly, p and q must be whole numbers 3 or bigger, and there exists a regular polygon with p sides for any such p — so, from the delicate way in which we settled the case {3, 5} (icosahedron), it is natural to fear that the general existence problem for regular solids must be hard. Surprisingly, this is not the case.

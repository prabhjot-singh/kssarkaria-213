Pyramid

The four topmost glass panels suggest a small pyramid with a square base and equilateral triangular sloping faces, but only two of these extend to those much bigger but similar sloping triangular walls of glass, so, more imaginatively, you might like to think in terms of a bigger pyramid of the same kind that has only managed to free itself partially from the confines of the edifice!

A quick continuity argument confirms that, if the apex is at a suitable height — in fact at height equal to the radius of the base — above the center of the square base, then the sloping and obviously isosceles triangular faces of the pyramid shall be equilateral.

The angle at the apex

For a variable pyramid with a q-sided regular polygon base and apex A moving up and down the perpendicular line through the center of the base, the angle α at the apex subtended by a polygonal edge takes all values less than 360/q degrees, and only these values.

As the apex A recedes towards infinity, α → 0. As A approaches the center of the base, α → 360 ÷ q. A regular solid {p, q} can exist only if there is a pyramid of the above kind with α equal to the interior angle of a regular p-sided polygon.

Five, and only five

A regular polygon with p sides can be cut up into p − 2 triangles, so the sum of its interior angles is (p − 2) × 180 degrees, so each is ((p − 2) × 180) / p degrees. Therefore, a regular solid {p, q} can exist only if (p − 2) × 180 ÷ p < 360 ÷ q. Rearranging gives a very strong constraint:

(q − 2)(p − 2) < 4

— that is, the product of the two whole numbers q − 2 and p − 2 must be less than four. So {p, q} = {3,3}, {3,4}, {4,3}, {3,5} or {5,3}.

The five {p, q}

Each cell shows (p − 2)(q − 2). The red cells satisfy the constraint and are the five Platonic solids. The tan cells along the edge satisfy equality, corresponding to the three regular planar tilings — tempting edge-cases we shall meet on the Magic Carpet.

In his own words: naming and duality

These five beauties are all there in this house! That "white-washed temple" on the rockery was a {3,3} (did you notice?), and we've discussed "Beesmukhi" = {3,5} at length, and this glass "Pyramid" (believe me!) is really the tip of a big {3,4} trying to free itself from the confines of this edifice. On the first-floor terrace, there are the "Eyes" or the skylights of that new and skew ground-floor living room. These depict the two pieces of a {4,3} (cube) that one obtains when it is cut perpendicularly to its main diagonal, and there is much of interest that can be said about these hexagonal sections, as well as the grills showing beneath them.

The {5,3} is also in the house, but as befits its special metaphysical significance, in a different avatar.

To mop up note that, given any {p, q} we can make a {q, p}: for each vertex of {p, q} take the regular polygon whose vertices are the q centers of the incident faces; the solid enclosed by all these polygons is a {q, p}. So by this method we can also make a {5, 3} or dodecahedron (twelve regular pentagonal faces, three at each vertex) and {4, 3} or hexahedron (six square faces, three at each vertex), respectively. The otherwise so rich English language has, very curiously, a popular synonym, cube, only for the regular solid {4, 3}.
